Curation Feeds

Jul 21, 2026

The Problem

Most of the content we consume is curated for us by algorithms that are designed to maximize engagement by analyzing all kinds of metrics about what we click on and how we interact with it. This naturally leads to an unhealthy system that tries to be as addictive as possible while incentivizing content creators to make mind-numbing emotion-driving garbage.

The solution that many people are arriving at is to take content curation into our own hands. Rather than just consuming whatever the algorithm places in front of us, we should be deliberate and intentional about finding producers of content we like and subscribing to them.

The problem with this approach is that people generally prefer to consume the best stuff from 500 content creators rather than the entire output of five creators. When you subscribe to someone, you get EVERYTHING they make, which is often more than we'd like. This is the problem that content curation algorithms solve for us. If we want a healthier human-powered content curation system, this is one of the problems that need to be solved.

The Solution

As an alternative to engagement-driven algorithms, people ought to create 'curation feeds' where they re-post the valuable content they find. There are many tools online that make this easy to do. Just find an online tool that lets you publish an RSS feed, then re-post every video, song, article, book, website, feed or thing that you find interesting.

For anyone who makes their own 'curation feed', I have three suggestions:

  1. You probably have several interests. If someone subscribes to your curation feed, they probably only have 1-2 interests in common with you, so make sure you divide your feed into catagories they can turn on or off from the comfort of their RSS reader.
  2. If you like to write blog posts, make music, or do some other creative thing, go ahead and add it to your curation feed, but put the stuff you make in separate catagories from the stuff you find. This lets people choose whether they want to subscribe to what you make or what you like or both.
  3. Don't feel limited about what types of content you can share on your curation feed. Physical books, cool stuff you bought on Amazon, inspirational quotes, a nice picture/meme, even other feeds you find--these are all great things to post on a curation feed.

If you want a concise way to introduce people to the idea of "curation feeds", you could copy the following example:

"As an alternative to engagement-driven algorithms, people ought to create 'curation feeds' where they re-post the valuable content they find. There are many tools online that make this easy to do." Here's mine: My curation feed.
